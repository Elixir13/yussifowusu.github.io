Diagnostic Accuracy of Rapid Antigen Tests for Malaria in Endemic Regions

A comprehensive meta-analysis evaluating the sensitivity and specificity of rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) for Plasmodium falciparum and non-falciparum malaria in field settings across 42 studies.

  • Python
  • R
  • SPSS
  • Meta Package (R)
  • PRISMA Guidelines

Project Overview

This project conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis to assess the diagnostic performance of malaria rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) in real-world endemic settings. The analysis synthesized data from diverse geographical regions, accounting for variations in transmission intensity, parasite species, and test brands.

Methodology

Studies were identified through systematic searches of PubMed, Scopus, and Cochrane databases following PRISMA guidelines. Statistical analysis employed bivariate random-effects models in R (meta package) to estimate pooled sensitivity and specificity, with subgroup analyses for parasite species and hrp2 deletions.

Results & Visualizations

Pooled sensitivity for P. falciparum was 94.8% (95% CI: 92.1–96.7%), with specificity of 95.2% (93.5–96.5%). Lower performance was observed in non-falciparum species and regions with hrp2 gene deletions. Summary receiver operating characteristic (SROC) curves and forest plots illustrated heterogeneity and diagnostic thresholds.

Outcomes & Impact

The findings, published in The Lancet Infectious Diseases (2024), provide updated evidence for WHO guidelines on malaria diagnostics and highlight the need for next-generation RDTs in hrp2-deleted parasite populations.
